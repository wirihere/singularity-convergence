What Does the Bible Say About Anger?

June 1, 2026 · 6 min read

Your face is hot. Your jaw is tight. Someone cut you off in traffic, or your boss took credit for your work again, or your spouse said the one thing they know cuts deepest. And now you're sitting there, heart pounding, replaying it over and over.

Then the voice arrives. The one you've heard since childhood. Good Christians don't get angry. Anger is a sin. You need to pray this away.

But here's the problem: that voice has never actually worked. You've prayed. You've counted to ten. You've tried to "let it go." And still — there it is. Hot. Real. Impossible to ignore. So either you're a terrible Christian, or everything you've been told about anger is wrong. The Bible has a clear answer. And it is not what most people think.

The Verse

"Be ye angry, and sin not: let not the sun go down upon your wrath: Neither give place to the devil." — Ephesians 4:26-27 (KJV)

The Story

Read those first four words again. Be ye angry. Not "never be angry." Not "anger is a sin you must eliminate." It's a command. An instruction. Be angry. The Apostle Paul, writing to the church in Ephesus, actually tells them to experience anger.

This is not a loophole. It's not a translation trick. The Greek word is orgizesthe — it's an imperative. Paul is giving an order. Get angry.

But then he immediately adds a boundary: "and sin not." The anger itself is not the problem. What you do with it is. You can be furious and still act righteously. You can feel the fire in your chest and still choose not to burn down the relationship.

And then Paul gives the most practical instruction in all of Scripture about emotions: "Let not the sun go down upon your wrath." Deal with it. Today. Before your head hits the pillow. Because anger is like raw meat — it's fine for a few hours, but leave it out overnight and it spoils. It becomes something else entirely. Bitterness. Resentment. A grudge that curls up in the corner of your heart and makes itself at home.

This is the pattern the Bible keeps repeating. Anger is not the enemy. Unprocessed anger is. Anger left to fester is. Anger that turns into contempt or violence or silent treatment — that's where the devil gets his foothold.

The Hidden Lesson

Most people read Ephesians 4:26 as a permission slip with fine print — "you can be angry, but don't mess up." The hidden lesson is much bigger than that. Anger is not a moral failure. It's a signal. And signals are morally neutral. A fire alarm is not "good" or "evil" — it's just telling you something is burning.

Think about what makes God angry in the Bible. Injustice. Oppression of the poor. Hypocrisy in religious leaders. The strong crushing the weak. God's anger is never random and never selfish. It's always a response to something that is genuinely wrong. And the Bible says you are made in God's image. That means your capacity for anger is not a design flaw — it's a feature. The question is what you're angry about.

Jesus himself got angry. Read Mark 3. A man with a withered hand is standing in the synagogue, and the religious leaders are watching Jesus to see if he'll heal on the Sabbath so they can accuse him. The verse says Jesus "looked round about on them with anger, being grieved for the hardness of their hearts." He was angry. At religious people. In church. On the Sabbath. And he didn't apologize for it. He healed the man anyway.

The hidden lesson is this: Anger can be holy. It can be righteous. It can be the exact right response to a broken world. What makes it sin is not the feeling — it's what happens next. Do you use it to destroy, or do you use it to heal? Do you nurse it into bitterness, or do you resolve it before the sun goes down?

And here's the part nobody tells you: suppressing anger doesn't make you righteous. It makes you sick. It goes underground and comes out sideways — as depression, as anxiety, as passive-aggressive comments you didn't mean to say. The Bible doesn't say "swallow your anger." It says "be angry" — feel it, name it, own it — "and sin not." Feel the feeling without letting the feeling drive the car.

For You

If you've been told your whole life that anger is a sin you need to kill, this might feel like permission you're not sure you deserve. It is. The Bible gives it to you. Your anger is not evidence that you're far from God. It might be evidence that you're paying attention to a world where children go hungry, where the innocent suffer, where people hurt each other every day. That anger is a God-given alarm system. Listen to it.

But here's the hard part: don't go to bed with it. Not because anger is bad. Because you matter too much to let something from today poison your tomorrow. Pray about it. Write it down. Say the hard words to the person who needs to hear them — gently, honestly, before the sun sets. The devil doesn't need a dramatic entrance. He just needs you to hold onto an offense one more night. And then another. And then another.

Going Deeper

The Bible doesn't give us one verse about anger and call it done. It gives us a whole instruction manual — because God knew this would be one of the hardest emotions for humans to navigate.

In Proverbs 29:11, Solomon writes: "A fool uttereth all his mind: but a wise man keepeth it in till afterwards." This is the ancient version of "count to ten." The fool blurts out every angry thought the moment it appears. The wise person lets the anger cool just long enough to decide what should be said. Not nothing. But not everything, either. The goal is not silence. The goal is timing.

James 1:19-20 adds another layer: "Wherefore, my beloved brethren, let every man be swift to hear, slow to speak, slow to wrath: For the wrath of man worketh not the righteousness of God." James draws a line between human anger and divine anger. God's anger produces justice. Human anger — when it's fast and thoughtless — produces chaos. The solution is not to eliminate anger. It's to slow it down. Listen first. Speak second. Get angry third. Most of us do it exactly backwards.

And then there's Psalm 37:8: "Cease from anger, and forsake wrath: fret not thyself in any wise to do evil." David isn't saying "never feel anger." He's saying "stop letting it live in you." There's a difference between a visitor and a tenant. Anger can knock on your door. You can even let it in for a conversation. But don't give it a key. Don't let it decorate the guest room. It's meant to visit, not to stay.
