What Does the Bible Say About Hell

April 7, 2026 · 6 min read

Ever feel like you’ve been sold a bill of goods? Like maybe, just maybe, some of the stuff you heard growing up in church was…a little off? Especially when it came to the fiery pits, the gnashing of teeth, and the eternal barbecue for bad people. Yeah, we're talking about hell. That word. That concept. The one that’s launched a thousand sermons and probably just as many sleepless nights.

Here's the thing: if you're like a lot of folks who’ve stepped back from traditional church, you might have noticed something. The whole "hell" narrative feels a bit…simple. A bit too much like a cosmic vending machine: put in good deeds, get heaven; put in bad deeds, get roasted. But does that really square with the God you sense in your gut? The one who’s supposed to be, you know, love?

What if I told you the word "hell" isn't even in the original Hebrew or Greek texts of the Bible? Mind blown, right? It's true. It's a translation choice. A powerful, impactful, and maybe even misleading translation choice. So, if it's not "hell," what *is* it? What was Jesus actually talking about when he spoke of judgment and consequences? Let’s crack open the good book and see what’s really bubbling under the surface.

The Verse

Let’s look at a classic, often used to warn about fiery consequences:

"But I say unto you, That whosoever is angry with his brother without a cause shall be in danger of the judgment: and whosoever shall say to his brother, Raca, shall be in danger of the council: but whosoever shall say, Thou fool, shall be in danger of hell fire." (Matthew 5:22 KJV)
"Hell fire." There it is. Or, rather, there it *isn't* in the original. The Greek word here is Gehenna. More on that in a bit.

The Story

Imagine it's the year 2024. You're living in a bustling city, maybe New York or London. You've got a neighbor, let's call him Dave. Dave's not a bad guy, but he's got this habit. He’s always complaining about everyone. Not just a little grumble, but full-blown, public rants. He'll stand on his balcony and yell about how the guy who parks his scooter on the sidewalk is a "total Raca"—an empty head, a brainless idiot. And if someone cuts him off in traffic, oh boy. He’s not just angry; he’s screaming, "You absolute fool! You deserve to lose your license!"

Now, in this modern city, there are consequences for this kind of behavior. If Dave just gets angry, maybe he’s in danger of a noise complaint from the building management – a "judgment" of sorts, a formal talking-to. If he starts calling people "Raca" to their faces, causing public disturbances, he might find himself in front of a community council or a mediator, trying to sort out neighborhood disputes. That's a step up, a more serious "council" intervention.

But what happens when Dave crosses a line? When his anger escalates into something truly toxic, something that poisons the entire community? When he's not just calling people names, but his words are actively tearing apart relationships, fostering hatred, maybe even inciting violence? Imagine his words lead to actual harm, or a total breakdown of trust on the block. The word "fool" here, in ancient context, was more than just an insult; it was a condemnation of someone’s character, an assault on their worth, suggesting they were morally bankrupt beyond repair.

In our modern city, if Dave's toxic words—his relentless shaming and dehumanizing of others—leads to a true social breakdown, where no one trusts anyone, where the very fabric of the community is ripped apart, what's the consequence? It's not just a fine. It’s not just a talking-to. It’s a total social meltdown. A place where meaningful connection, peace, and shared life simply can't exist. It’s like a spiritual landfill, a place where all the garbage of our broken human interactions ends up, burning away all possibility of flourishing.

The Hidden Lesson

Here's the kicker about Gehenna, that word often translated as "hell fire" in Matthew 5:22. Gehenna wasn't some mythical, underground torture chamber. It was a real place. A valley just outside Jerusalem. And it had a reputation. A dark, gnarly history. It was where child sacrifices were once offered to the god Molech. Gross, right? Later, it became a garbage dump. A place where everything undesirable, everything unclean, everything that needed to be destroyed, was thrown. And yeah, they kept fires burning there to consume the trash, to keep the stench down, to deal with the decay. It was a place of perpetual burning, not as eternal torture for people, but as relentless destruction of filth.

So when Jesus talks about Gehenna, he's not inventing a new cosmic furnace. He's using a very real, very vivid image that his listeners would have understood instantly. He’s saying, "Look, your anger, your dehumanizing words, your toxic judgments—they don't just hurt someone's feelings. They create a spiritual Gehenna. They create a place of absolute waste and destruction. They turn good, healthy relationships into garbage that needs to be burned away."

The "fire" isn't about God actively torturing you. It’s about the natural, destructive consequences of your actions. It's about a place where things are consumed, where they cease to be. It’s the ultimate consequence of living a life that produces nothing but spiritual garbage. It’s not eternal punishment *from* God, but the eternal *result* of choosing to live in a way that’s completely contrary to life, love, and connection. It’s what happens when you reduce people to "Raca" or "fool"—you're effectively throwing them, and yourself, into the spiritual garbage dump.

For You

So, if you’ve been wrestling with the idea of a good God sending people to an eternal barbecue, take a breath. The picture Jesus painted was far more nuanced, far more rooted in real-world consequences and spiritual decay. It's less about a punitive deity and more about the inevitable outcome of our choices. It's about recognizing that our words, our anger, our judgments, have real weight. They don't just float off into the ether. They can create a literal hell on earth, and within us, a place where spiritual life just can't flourish. That's a pretty heavy thought, isn't it? But also, maybe, a liberating one.

Going Deeper

Want to dig a little more into this idea of consequences and spiritual decay?

* **Matthew 10:28:** "And fear not them which kill the body, but are not able to kill the soul: but rather fear him which is able to destroy both soul and body in Gehenna." Here, Jesus isn't talking about eternal suffering, but destruction. Like that old VHS player in the garage that finally gave up the ghost. It's gone. Destroyed. What a concept for a God of love, right? To *destroy* what's broken, not just endlessly torment it. * **James 3:6:** "And the tongue is a fire, a world of iniquity: so is the tongue among our members, that it defileth the whole body, and setteth on fire the course of nature; and it is set on fire of Gehenna." James gets super real about the power of our words. He connects the destructive power of the tongue directly to Gehenna, emphasizing that the "fire" often starts with us, with our venomous words and attitudes. It's like a wildfire, but for your soul and relationships. * **Isaiah 66:24:** This Old Testament passage describes a place outside Jerusalem where "their worm shall not die, neither shall their fire be quenched." This is often cited as a proof text for eternal torment. But it's talking about the *corpses* of rebels, not living souls. It's a picture of utter degradation and destruction, a public display of the end of those who reject God. Think of it less as a horror movie and more as a very vivid cautionary tale from ancient times.
