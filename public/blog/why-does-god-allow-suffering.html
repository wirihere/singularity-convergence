Why Does God Allow Suffering?

May 28, 2026 · 6 min read

You've heard it. Someone you love dies. A child gets cancer. A war destroys a whole city. And then someone — maybe a well-meaning friend, maybe a pastor — says: "It's all part of God's plan."

And something inside you snaps. Because if this is the plan, then maybe the plan is broken. Maybe God isn't good. Maybe God isn't there at all.

You're not alone in asking this. It's the oldest question in religion. And the Bible doesn't give a neat, tidy answer. What it gives is something much more honest — and much more useful.

The Verse

"Though he slay me, yet will I trust in him: but I will maintain mine own ways before him." — Job 13:15 (KJV)

The Story

There's a man in the Bible named Job. He's the richest guy in town. Seven thousand sheep. Three thousand camels. Ten kids. A wife. A reputation for being the most righteous man alive. God himself brags about Job to Satan — "Have you seen my servant Job? There's no one like him on earth."

And then God lets Satan take everything.

Let that sink in. God doesn't cause the suffering, but He allows it. He gives Satan permission. In one day, Job loses his livestock, his servants, and all ten of his children. A wind collapses the house they were eating in. All of them dead. Then Job himself gets covered in painful sores from head to toe.

Job's wife tells him to curse God and die. His three best friends show up and spend thirty-five chapters explaining why this is all Job's fault. "You must have sinned," they say. "God rewards the righteous and punishes the wicked. So what did you do?"

They sit in the ashes with him for seven days without saying a word. And then they open their mouths and make everything worse.

Job refuses their logic. He knows he didn't do anything to deserve this. And he demands an answer from God. Not politely. He yells. He accuses. He says, "I want to take God to court. I want to present my case. I want to know why."

And here's the part that should make every religious person uncomfortable: God doesn't punish Job for saying this.

The Hidden Lesson

When God finally answers Job — after 37 chapters of silence — He doesn't explain the suffering. Not at all. Instead, God shows up in a whirlwind and asks Job a series of questions: "Where were you when I laid the foundations of the earth? Have you commanded the morning since your days began? Can you bind the chains of the Pleiades?"

God's response is basically: "You don't have the context to understand what I'm doing."

Most people read this as God putting Job in his place. "Shut up, human. You're too small to understand." But there's a hidden lesson here that almost everyone misses: God showed up.

Job's friends — the religious ones, the ones with all the answers — they talked about God for 35 chapters. They had charts. They had theology. They had explanations. And they were wrong about every single thing. At the end of the book, God tells them: "You have not spoken of me what is right, as my servant Job has."

Job, who screamed at God and demanded answers — he was the one who spoke rightly. Not because he had the right explanation. But because he was honest. Because he refused to pretend the suffering made sense. Because he took his pain directly to God instead of burying it under religious cliches.

The hidden lesson isn't "suffering has a purpose." The hidden lesson is that God would rather have your honest anger than your fake piety. He'd rather you yell at Him than stop talking to Him altogether.

And here's the other thing most people miss: God never tells Job why this happened. The book ends with Job getting twice as much as he had before — but he never gets an explanation. The suffering happened. The loss was real. And the answer to "why" is simply: you don't get to know.

That sounds cruel until you realize what it means. It means God isn't looking for employees who understand the business plan. He's looking for partners who trust Him even when the numbers don't add up.

For You

If you're in pain right now and someone tells you "everything happens for a reason," you have my permission to ignore them. That's not what the Bible teaches. The Bible teaches that suffering is real, that it's okay to be angry about it, and that the people who pretend to have answers are usually the ones who understand the least.

Your suffering isn't punishment. It's not a test you're failing. And you don't have to pretend you're okay. Job didn't. David didn't — read Psalm 22 and count how many times he says "why have you forsaken me." Jesus quoted that exact psalm from the cross. If the Son of God can ask "why," so can you.

Going Deeper

The Bible doesn't just give us Job. It gives us a whole chorus of people asking the same question you're asking.

In Romans 8:18, Paul writes: "For I reckon that the sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory which shall be revealed in us." Notice what Paul doesn't say. He doesn't say suffering is good. He doesn't say you should be happy about it. He says it's real — but it's temporary. The comparison isn't "suffering is nothing." The comparison is "what's coming is so much bigger that the suffering will look small in hindsight."

And in 2 Corinthians 1:3-4, Paul makes a fascinating claim: God "comforteth us in all our tribulation, that we may be able to comfort them which are in any trouble." In other words: your suffering isn't wasted. The comfort you receive becomes something you can give away. Every person who's walked through fire has a flashlight for the people still inside.

Finally, consider Revelation 21:4: "And God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes; and there shall be no more death, neither sorrow, nor crying, neither shall there be any more pain." This is the end of the story. Not an explanation for present suffering, but a promise about its expiration date. Pain has a shelf life. It won't last forever. The Bible doesn't tell you why it exists. It tells you it won't always.
