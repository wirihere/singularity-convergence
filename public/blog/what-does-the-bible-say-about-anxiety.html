What Does the Bible Say About Anxiety?

April 7, 2026 · 6 min read

If you've ever sat in a church pew with your chest tight and your thoughts racing, you've probably heard someone say: "Just pray about it." Or maybe they quoted Philippians at you like it was a magic spell. Cast your anxiety on the Lord. Be anxious for nothing. Just trust God.

And you sat there thinking: I am trusting God. I'm also having a panic attack.

Here's the thing most churches won't tell you: the Bible doesn't say "stop being anxious." It says something way more interesting than that.

The Verse

"Be careful for nothing; but in every thing by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known unto God. And the peace of God, which passeth all understanding, shall keep your hearts and minds through Christ Jesus." — Philippians 4:6-7 (KJV)

The Story

Picture a kid holding a glass of water. Not heavy at all. But tell that kid to hold the glass straight out with their arm extended. After five minutes, it aches. After twenty minutes, their arm is shaking. After an hour? They drop it.

The glass didn't get heavier. The kid just never put it down.

That's anxiety. It's not that your problems are too big. It's that you never set them down. You carry them into the shower, into bed, into every conversation. You replay the worst-case scenario like a movie you've already seen forty times. Your brain knows the ending but it hits play again anyway.

Paul — the guy who wrote Philippians — wasn't sitting in a comfortable house when he wrote that verse. He was in prison. Actual chains. Actual guards. He had real things to be anxious about. He wasn't giving advice from a place of comfort. He was demonstrating something from inside a cell.

The Hidden Lesson

Here's what most churches skip right over.

Paul doesn't say "be anxious for nothing" as a command. Read it again. He's not yelling at you to stop worrying. The Greek word there — merimnao — means to be pulled in different directions. It's the image of a person being torn apart. Paul is saying: don't let yourself be ripped in half by this.

And his solution isn't "stop feeling things." His solution is to redirect. He says: take that same energy — that anxious, spinning, can't-sleep energy — and aim it somewhere. Prayer. Supplication. Thanksgiving. He's not saying shut off the engine. He's saying point the car somewhere useful.

Anxiety isn't a sin. It's not a failure of faith. David had it — read Psalm 55. Elijah had it so bad he asked God to kill him. Jesus himself sweat blood in the Garden of Gethsemane from the pressure of what was coming. If anxiety was a sin, then Jesus sinned. And we both know that's not how the story goes.

The real lesson? Anxiety is energy pointed at the wrong target. The Bible doesn't tell you to kill the energy. It tells you to redirect it.

For You

If your chest is tight right now, if the thoughts won't stop looping, if someone told you that real Christians don't struggle with anxiety — they lied to you. Not on purpose, probably. But they lied. The Bible is full of anxious people who God called heroes. You're in better company than anyone told you.

Going Deeper

The Bible doesn't mention anxiety once and move on. It keeps coming back to it, because the writers knew this was a human constant.

In Matthew 6:25-27, Jesus asks a question that sounds almost sarcastic: "Which of you by taking thought can add one cubit unto his stature?" He's basically saying — has worrying ever actually made you taller? Has it solved a single problem? It's not a rebuke. It's an honest question. And the honest answer is no.

In Psalm 55:22, David writes: "Cast thy burden upon the Lord, and he shall sustain thee." Notice he doesn't say the burden disappears. He says you'll be sustained through it. There's a massive difference between "God will take your anxiety away" and "God will hold you up while you carry it." The second one is what the Bible actually promises.

And in 1 Peter 5:7: "Casting all your care upon him; for he careth for you." The word "casting" there is the same word used for throwing a net into the sea. It's not a gentle hand-off. It's a throw. Peter is saying: hurl it. Don't politely offer your anxiety to God like you're passing a plate at dinner. Throw it. That's how heavy it is, and that's how seriously God takes it.

The pattern across all these verses is the same: anxiety is real, it's heavy, and the solution isn't to pretend it doesn't exist. The solution is to stop carrying it alone.
