What Does the Bible Say About Love?

July 15, 2026 · 6 min read

There's a reading at almost every wedding you've ever been to. 1 Corinthians 13. "Love is patient, love is kind." People cry. The bride's mom dabs her eyes. Then everybody goes to the reception and forgets what it said by the time the cake is cut.

Here's the problem. That chapter wasn't written for weddings. Paul wrote it for a church that was falling apart. And if you actually read the sentence right before it, the whole thing means something completely different from what you think.

The Verse

"Though I speak with the tongues of men and of angels, and have not charity, I am become as sounding brass, or a tinkling cymbal. And though I have the gift of prophecy, and understand all mysteries, and all knowledge; and though I have all faith, so that I could remove mountains, and have not charity, I am nothing." — 1 Corinthians 13:1-2 (KJV)

The Story

Corinth was a mess. The church there had split into factions. One group followed Paul. Another followed a guy named Apollos. Another claimed to follow Peter. They were competing over who had the best spiritual gifts, who could speak in tongues the loudest, who had the most impressive prophecies. It was a popularity contest dressed up as worship.

Paul writes them a letter. And right before the famous "love chapter," he says something most people skip: "And though I have the gift of prophecy, and understand all mysteries, and all knowledge; and though I have all faith, so that I could remove mountains, and have not charity, I am nothing."

Think about that. You could move mountains. You could understand every mystery in the universe. You could speak the language of angels. Without love, Paul says you're nothing. Not "you're less." Not "you're behind." Nothing. A gong. An empty cymbal making noise.

He's talking to people who were obsessed with being impressive. Sound familiar? We live in a world that measures you by what you produce, what you achieve, how many followers you have. The Corinthians were the first influencers, and Paul is telling them: you can have every gift in the world and it counts for zero without love.

Notice something else. The word Paul uses isn't the warm fuzzy word for love. He doesn't use eros, which is romantic love. He doesn't use phileo, which is brotherly affection. He uses agape, which is something different entirely. Agape is a choice. It's a decision to act in someone's best interest even when they don't deserve it and even when it costs you. It's not a feeling. It's a direction you point your will.

The Hidden Lesson

Here's what nearly everybody misses.

When Paul lists what love is and isn't, he's not describing a personality type. He's not saying "nice people do this, mean people don't." Read it carefully. Love endures. Love bears all things. Love hopes all things. These are verbs. Actions. Things you do when every part of you wants to do something else.

Love enduring isn't the same as love feeling good. Love bearing all things isn't the same as love being easy. Paul is describing what love does in the trenches, not what it looks like on a wedding day. He's writing to people who were actively making each other miserable. And he's saying: the only thing that fixes this is choosing to act in each other's best interest when you'd rather not.

There's a reason Paul puts this chapter right in the middle of his teaching on spiritual gifts. He's saying: you're arguing about who has the best gifts, and you've completely missed the point. The gifts are tools. Love is the purpose. You can have all the tools in the world, but if you don't know what you're building, you're just making noise.

For You

If you've ever felt like love was supposed to feel a certain way and you're failing at it, read the chapter again. Paul never once says love feels like anything. He says love does things. It is patient. It is kind. It doesn't envy. It doesn't boast. These aren't feelings. They're decisions you make on a Tuesday when someone annoys you and you choose not to snap back.

That's actually good news. Because feelings come and go like weather. But a decision you can make again tomorrow.

Going Deeper

The Bible talks about love more than almost any other topic, and it keeps coming back to the same pattern: love is action, not emotion.

In John 15:13, Jesus says: "Greater love hath no man than this, that a man lay down his life for his friends." Notice he says "lay down," not "feel warmth toward." He's talking about sacrifice. The ultimate love is the one that costs something. If your love doesn't cost you anything, Jesus is suggesting you haven't gotten to the real thing yet.

In 1 John 4:8, it gets even more direct: "He that loveth not knoweth not God; for God is love." John doesn't say "God feels loving." He says God is love. It's not something God does on the side. It's what God is. And if you want to know what God looks like, John is saying, look at what love does, not what it says.

And in Micah 6:8, the prophet breaks it down to the simplest possible terms: "What doth the Lord require of thee, but to do justly, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with thy God?" Love mercy. Not just show it. Not just agree with the concept of it. Love it so much that you practice it even when the other person didn't earn it.

The Bible's picture of love is the opposite of what the world sells you. The world says love is a spark you find. The Bible says love is a fire you build, one choice at a time, even when the wood is wet and the wind is against you.
