What Does the Bible Say About Forgiveness?

July 13, 2026 · 6 min read

If you've ever sat in a church counseling session with someone who hurt you, you've probably heard the phrase: \"Forgive and forget.\" It sounds nice in theory. But in practice, it feels like being told to pretend the wound never happened. Like you're the one who has to do the work of erasing reality.

And you sat there thinking: I can forgive them. But I can't forget what they did. Does that mean I'm doing it wrong?

Here's the thing most churches won't tell you: the Bible never says \"forgive and forget.\" Not once. It says something way more interesting than that.

The Verse

\"Then came Peter to him, and said, Lord, how oft shall my brother sin against me, and I forgive him? till seven times? Jesus saith unto him, I say not unto thee, Until seven times: but, Until seventy times seven.\" — Matthew 18:21-22 (KJV)

The Story

Jesus just told a parable about a guy who owed a king millions of dollars. The king forgave the entire debt — wiped it clean. That same guy then went out and found a coworker who owed him twenty bucks. He choked the coworker, threw him in prison, demanded every penny. When the king heard, he was furious. \"I forgave you all that debt. Couldn't you do the same for him?\"

The story ends with the unforgiving servant getting thrown into prison himself. Jesus's punchline is clear: the way you treat others is the way God treats you.

But here's the part everyone skips. Before Peter even asked his question, he'd already been watching Jesus handle betrayal. Judas was stealing from the ministry fund for months. The disciples knew. Jesus knew. And he kept Judas around. He washed Judas's feet at the Last Supper. He broke bread with him. He looked him in the eye knowing what was coming.

That's the context for Peter's question. He's not asking a theoretical question about forgiveness. He's asking: How much betrayal are we supposed to tolerate?

The Hidden Lesson

Here's what most churches skip right over.

Jesus doesn't say \"forgive seventy times seven\" as a math problem. He's not saying 490 times and then you're done. The Hebrew way of speaking, the cultural context there — saying a number seven times is poetic. It means limitlessness. Complete. Abundant. More than you can count.

And here's the twist: forgiveness in the Bible is not about forgetting. It's not about suppressing your memory. It's not about pretending the hurt didn't happen. God never tells you to lie about reality.

Forgiveness is about releasing someone from the debt they owe you. Think about that servant who owed millions. The king didn't forget the debt. The king chose to absorb the cost himself. That's what forgiveness costs — you absorb what was owed to you.

The Bible doesn't say \"forget it happened.\" It says \"don't hold it against them.\" Those are different. You can remember what someone did while still choosing not to keep an account. You can acknowledge the wound while refusing to weaponize it.

The real lesson? Forgiveness isn't about your memory. It's about your ledger. And God says: throw away the ledger.

For You

If you're carrying a list of who owes you an apology, who needs to pay for what they did — you're the one paying the interest. Every time you replay what they did, you're paying. Every time you bring it up in arguments, you're paying. Forgiveness isn't letting them off the hook. It's letting yourself off the hook. The debt was real. The cost was real. But you don't have to keep paying it forever.

Going Deeper

The Bible has a lot to say about forgiveness because it knows how hard we are. It knows this isn't something you do once and you're done.

In Colossians 3:13, Paul writes: \"Forgiving one another, if any man have a quarrel against any: even as Christ forgave you, so also do ye.\" The Greek word for forgive there — charizomai — literally means to give freely or to bestow a favor. Forgiveness isn't a transaction. It's a gift. You give it not because they earned it. You give it because you were given it first.

In Ephesians 4:32: \"And be ye kind one to another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, even as God for Christ's sake hath forgiven you.\" Notice the pattern: always, always, always, forgiveness in the Bible is presented as something you received first. You're not inventing forgiveness. You're passing along what you already got.

And in Mark 11:25, Jesus says something that sounds almost harsh: \"And when ye stand praying, forgive, if ye have ought against any: that your Father also which is in heaven may forgive you your trespasses.\" The connection isn't subtle. Your forgiveness is connected to God's forgiveness. Not because God is transactional. But because forgiveness is what opens you to receive it. If you're clutching a debt list, your hands aren't open to receive anything else.

The pattern across all these verses is the same: forgiveness is hard, it costs something, and it's how you access the same grace you've already been given. The Bible never tells you to pretend the hurt didn't happen. It tells you to stop keeping score.
