What Does the Bible Say About Forgiveness?

May 28, 2026 · 6 min read

You've probably heard it a hundred times. "You need to forgive." "Just let it go." "Forgive and forget."

And every time someone says that to you, there's a voice inside your head that screams: You don't understand what they did to me.

Here's the thing nobody tells you: the Bible doesn't actually say "forgive and forget." That phrase isn't in there. What the Bible does say about forgiveness is way more practical, way more honest, and way harder than most people realize. But it's also the only thing that actually works.

The Verse

"Then came Peter to him, and said, Lord, how oft shall my brother sin against me, and I forgive him? till seven times? Jesus saith unto him, I say not unto thee, Until seven times: but, Until seventy times seven." — Matthew 18:21-22 (KJV)

The Story

Picture a man carrying a backpack. Not a normal backpack — this one is full of bricks. Every brick has a name on it. The person who lied to him. The boss who humiliated him. The friend who betrayed him. The parent who wasn't there.

He's been carrying this backpack for years. His shoulders ache. His back is bent. He can't run anymore. He can barely walk. But every time someone suggests he set the backpack down, he tightens the straps and says: "You don't understand. They put these bricks there. They owe me."

And he's right. They did put the bricks there. They do owe him. But here's the question nobody asks: who's carrying the weight?

Peter came to Jesus with what he thought was a generous offer. Seven times. The rabbis of the day taught you only had to forgive three times. Peter doubled it and added one. He was basically saying: "I'll be extra righteous."

And Jesus looked at him and basically said: Peter, you're still counting. That's the problem.

The Hidden Lesson

Here's what most sermons skip right over.

Jesus didn't give Peter the number 490 because he wanted Peter to keep a tally sheet. He gave a number so big you'd lose count before you got there. That's the whole point. If you're still counting, you haven't actually forgiven. You're just keeping score with nicer handwriting.

And here's the deeper thing: forgiveness in the Bible isn't about the other person at all. It's about you. It's not saying what they did was okay. It's not pretending it didn't happen. It's not even reconciling with them — Paul says in Romans 12:18, "If it be possible, as much as lieth in you, live peaceably with all men." Notice that: if it be possible. Sometimes it's not. Sometimes the healthiest thing you can do is forgive someone and never speak to them again.

Forgiveness is the decision to stop letting someone live rent-free in your head. It's saying: "What you did was wrong. It still matters. But I'm not going to let it be the director of my life anymore. I'm giving the keys back to myself."

The Greek word Jesus uses — aphiemi — literally means "to send away" or "to let go." It's the same word used for releasing a prisoner. When you forgive, you're not setting the other person free. You're setting yourself free.

For You

If there's someone whose name makes your stomach tighten, if there's a memory that plays on repeat when you're trying to sleep, you're not weak for struggling with this. Forgiveness isn't a light switch. It's a process. And sometimes you have to forgive the same thing seventy times seven — not because the other person deserves it seventy times, but because it takes that many repetitions to truly let it go.

God never asks you to pretend the wound wasn't real. He asks you to stop picking at it.

Going Deeper

The Bible doesn't mention forgiveness once and move on. It's woven through the entire story, because the writers knew this was the hardest thing a human being ever has to do.

In Colossians 3:13, Paul writes: "Forbearing one another, and forgiving one another, if any man have a quarrel against any: even as Christ forgave you, so also do ye." Notice the order. Paul says Christ forgave you first. Your forgiveness of others flows out of the forgiveness you've already received. It's not about earning anything. It's about passing on what you've already been given.

In Ephesians 4:31-32: "Let all bitterness, and wrath, and anger, and clamour, and evil speaking, be put away from you, with all malice: and be ye kind one to another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another." That list isn't random. Bitterness comes first because bitterness is what happens when you don't forgive. It's the rot that sets in when you leave a wound open. Paul's not just telling you to forgive — he's telling you what happens if you don't.

And then there's Luke 23:34. Jesus is literally nailed to a cross. The soldiers are gambling for his clothes at his feet. And he says: "Father, forgive them; for they know not what they do." If anyone had the right to hold a grudge, it was the guy being murdered by the people he came to save. And he forgave them in the middle of it — not after the pain was over, not once he'd healed. Right there, while it was happening.

The pattern across all these verses is the same: forgiveness isn't a feeling. It's a choice. And it's not a gift you give to the person who hurt you. It's a gift you give to yourself.
